Smishing Information

What is Smishing?

Smishing (SMS phishing) is a scam where criminals send fraudulent text messages pretending to be from legitimate companies or organizations to steal your personal information or install malware on your device. These scams often target:

  • Bank account and credit card information
  • Login credentials for online accounts
  • Personal identification details
  • Payment through gift cards or money transfers

Smishing attacks have increased by 328% in recent years as more people rely on text messaging for communication.

How Smishing Works

Scammers use sophisticated techniques to make their text messages appear legitimate:

  1. Spoofed sender IDs: Messages appear to come from familiar numbers or shortcodes
  2. Urgent requests: Claims your account is compromised or package can't be delivered
  3. Malicious links: Shortened URLs that lead to fake login pages
  4. Fake customer service: Requests to call fraudulent support numbers
  5. Malware downloads: Attachments that install spyware or ransomware

The goal is to bypass your natural suspicion by creating a sense of urgency or importance.

Example of a Smishing Text

Here's a realistic example of a bank-related smishing attempt:

Chase Alert: Unusual $1,200 charge from Amazon. Reply Y to confirm or N to deny. If unrecognized, secure your account: chase-secure.live/account

Red Flags in This Message:

  1. Suspicious link: "chase-secure.live" isn't Chase's real domain (should be chase.com)
  2. Urgent language: Designed to panic you into acting without thinking
  3. Request for reply: Legitimate banks don't verify transactions this way
  4. Generic greeting: Doesn't use your name despite claiming to be your bank

Common Smishing Scenarios

Be especially wary of these frequent smishing scams:

1. Bank Fraud Alerts

Messages claiming suspicious activity on your account with a link to "verify" your information.

2. Package Delivery Issues

Texts about undeliverable packages with links to "reschedule delivery" or pay fees.

3. Account Suspension Warnings

Threats that your Netflix/Amazon/PayPal account will be suspended unless you update payment info.

4. COVID-19 Related Scams

Fake contact tracing alerts or vaccine appointment confirmations with malicious links.

5. Prize/Giveaway Scams

Messages claiming you've won a prize but need to pay shipping fees or provide personal details.

How to Protect Yourself

Follow these guidelines to avoid falling victim to smishing scams:

Safety Tip

Never click links in unsolicited text messages - even if they appear to come from companies you trust. Always navigate to websites directly through your browser.

Best Practices for SMS Security

  • Verify before responding: Contact the organization directly using a known phone number or website
  • Check URLs carefully: Hover over links (on desktop) or long-press (on mobile) to see the actual destination
  • Don't reply: Even texting "STOP" can confirm your number is active to scammers
  • Enable spam protection: Use built-in spam filtering on your smartphone
  • Update regularly: Keep your phone's OS and messaging apps updated for security patches

Technical Protections

  • Install a reputable mobile security app with smishing protection
  • Use different passwords for each online account
  • Enable two-factor authentication (but not via SMS when possible)
  • Regularly review your bank and credit card statements

Verification Tip

If unsure about a message, search online for the exact wording - most scams use templates that others have reported.

What to Do If You Responded to Smishing

If you clicked a link or shared information:

  1. Disconnect your device from WiFi and mobile data immediately
  2. Change passwords for any accounts you accessed
  3. Contact financial institutions if you entered payment details
  4. Run antivirus scans if you downloaded anything
  5. Forward the message to 7726 (SPAM) to report it to your carrier
  6. File a report with the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov
